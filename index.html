Module 1

Reflection

Reflection happens when light strikes a surface and bounces back into the original medium. For a smooth surface, the law of reflection states that the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection, with both angles measured from the normal.

Plane mirrors do not focus light. They produce upright, virtual images the same distance behind the mirror as the object is in front of it. The ray model is the starting point for every larger geometric optics system.

Specular reflection Normal line Virtual image

Key Rule

theta_i = theta_r

The reflected ray stays in the same plane as the incident ray and the normal.

Module 2

Refraction and Different Media

Refraction occurs because light changes speed when it crosses into a new medium. The index of refraction, n, describes how strongly a medium slows light relative to vacuum.

If light enters a medium with a larger index, it bends toward the normal. If it enters a smaller index, it bends away from the normal. When light tries to leave a high-index medium at a large enough angle, total internal reflection can occur.

Snell's law Critical angle Total internal reflection

Key Rule

n1 sin(theta1) = n2 sin(theta2)

Compare the indices and angle to predict whether the ray bends toward or away from the normal.

Module 3

Curved Mirrors

Concave mirrors converge parallel rays toward a focal point and can create real or virtual images depending on object position. Convex mirrors diverge rays and always produce upright, reduced, virtual images.

The thin mirror equation predicts the image position, while magnification describes how image size compares to object size. Ray diagrams explain why the math works.

Concave Convex Magnification

Key Rules

m = -di/do = hi/ho

Module 4

Lenses

Converging lenses bend parallel rays toward a focal point on the far side of the lens. Diverging lenses spread rays apart as if they came from a focal point on the same side as the object.

The thin lens equation has the same form as the mirror equation, but the geometry changes because rays pass through the optic instead of reflecting from it. Central rays continue straight through the middle of the lens.

Converging lens Diverging lens Real vs. virtual image

Key Rules

Module 5

Interference and Diffraction

When light behaves like a wave, path difference matters. Two slits create bright fringes where crests and troughs line up, and dark fringes where they cancel. A single slit creates a broad central maximum and weaker side maxima because each part of the slit acts like a source.

Double-slit interference depends strongly on slit separation, while diffraction depends strongly on slit width. Wavelength and screen distance stretch or compress the pattern in both cases.

Constructive interference Destructive interference Diffraction envelope

Key Rules

d sin(theta) = m lambda

a sin(theta) = m lambda

Use the first for double slit bright fringes and the second for diffraction minima.

Module 6

Polarization

Polarization describes the direction in which the electric field oscillates. Unpolarized light contains many directions. A polarizing filter only transmits the component aligned with its transmission axis.

After light passes one polarizer, a second polarizer changes the transmitted intensity depending on the relative angle between their axes. This is the basis of glare reduction, LCD displays, and many optical sensors.

Transmission axis Malus's law Intensity filtering

Key Rule

I = I0 cos^2(theta)

For unpolarized light, the first ideal polarizer reduces the intensity to one-half before the analyzer angle matters.
